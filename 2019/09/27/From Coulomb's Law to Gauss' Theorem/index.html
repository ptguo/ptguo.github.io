From Coulomb's Law to Gauss' Theorem

Posted on 2019-09-27 Edited on 2020-01-13 In Electromagnetism

Coulomb’s law is a mathematical description of the electrostatic interaction between two point charges. It is very convenient to deal with the electrostatic force between points. However, when we talk about electromagnetic fields, we focus more on integrity, such as flux and circulation. This paper deduces Gauss’s theorem and circuital theorem from Coulomb’s law and elaborates that they are different reflections of the same physical law in the whole and part.

From Coulomb’s Law to Gauss’ Theorem

Coulomb’s Law is written as
$$
F=\frac{Qq}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2}
$$
The electric field strength is expressed as
$$
\vec E=\frac{q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2}\vec e_r
$$
Perform arbitrary surface integration on the field strength. According to the mathematical Gauss theorem, convert the surface integration to volume fraction, and get
$$
\iint_\Omega \vec E\cdot d\vec S=\iiint\nabla\cdot\vec EdV=\frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\iiint\nabla\cdot\frac{\vec e_r}{r^2}dV
$$
, where $\Omega$ is the surface that arbitrarily wraps the source charge. In mathematics,
$$
\nabla\cdot\frac{\vec e_r}{r^2}=4\pi\delta^3(x,y,z)
$$
where, $\delta^3(x,y,z)$ is the three-dimensional Dirac delta function. According to the remainder theorem in the complex function, it is easy to calculate the volume
$$
\iiint\delta^3(x,y,z)dV=1
$$
. As a result, for any closed surface,
$$
\iint_\Omega \vec E\cdot d\vec S=\frac{q}{\varepsilon_0}
$$
. Due to the superposition of the field, when there are multiple point charges,
$$
\iint_\Omega \vec E\cdot d\vec S=\frac{\sum_i q_i}{\varepsilon_0}
$$
. This is the Gauss theorem in physics.

From Coulomb’s Law to circuital theorem

In the same way, it can be proved that the ring quantity of the electrostatic field is zero.
$$
\oint\vec E\cdot d\vec l=\frac{q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\oint\frac{\vec e_r}{r^2}\cdot d\vec l
$$
According to Stokes theorem, the curve integral can be converted into a surface integral.
$$
\oint\frac{\vec e_r}{r^2}\cdot d\vec l=\iint\left(\nabla\times\frac{\vec e_r}{r^2}\right)\cdot d\vec S
$$
In a spherical coordinate system,
$$
\nabla\times\frac{\vec e_r}{r^2}=\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial r}\vec e_r+\frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial}{\partial\phi}\vec e_\phi+\frac{1}{r\sin\theta}\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\vec e_\theta\right)\times\frac{\vec e_r}{r^2}=0
$$
So for any closed curve
$$
\oint\vec E\cdot d\vec l=0
$$

Why $\iiint\nabla\cdot\frac{\vec e_r}{r^2}dV=4\pi$ ?

The following briefly proves that the integral of the Dirac delta function is 1. In a spherical coordinate system, when $r\neq0$,
$$
\nabla\cdot\frac{\vec e_r}{r^2}=\frac{1}{r^2\sin\theta}\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial r}r^2\sin\theta\frac{1}{r^2}\right)=0
$$
. When $r=R\to 0$, integrate a sphere with a radius of R at O,
$$
\lim_{R\to 0}\iiint_{r\leq R}\nabla\cdot\frac{\vec e_r}{r^2}dV=\lim_{R\to 0}\iint_{r=R}\frac{\vec e_r}{r^2}\cdot d\vec S=\lim_{R\to 0}\frac{1}{R^2}\iint\vec e_r\cdot\vec e_rdS
$$
$$
=\lim_{R\to 0}\frac{1}{R^2}\iint dS=\lim_{R\to 0}\frac{4\pi R^2}{R^2}=4\pi
$$
So as long as you include $ r = 0 $, the result is $4\pi$. The Dirac delta function is defined as a special function with O integral of 1 and 0 elsewhere. Therefore, the above divergence can be expressed as a Dirac delta function,
$$
\nabla\cdot\frac{\vec e_r}{r^2}=4\pi\delta^3(x,y,z)
$$
